Research/Programming

National Defense Science and Engineering Graduate (NDSEG) Research Fellow: Computational Physics (Active Matter)

UNC-Chapel Hill: Klotsa Group

Using C++ and molecular dynamics models, I simulate active matter systems of self-propelled particles that phase separate into a dense cluster and a dilute gas phase. Using algorithms I write in Python, I analyze big data to understand how properties of the individual agents contribute to the dynamic, collective behavior of the whole. Finally, I derive novel mathematical theories predicated on fundamental physics and non-equilibrium statistical mechanics that explain and predict the observed collective behavior.


Aiming to be well-rounded, my graduate school career was a juggling act of interdisciplinary roles: researcher, teacher, mentor, advocate, leader, and the list goes on. As more and more responsibilities got thrown into my hands, it was only natural to drop a ball here and there but picking it back up was key while I adjust and improve. From both my successes and failures, I've become confident in my ability to not only face a challenge but also develop and execute a plan to overcome unforseen problems: I know how to analyze and mitigate risk, adapt plans while meeting rigid deadlines, and collaborate with others of diverse expertise to solve problems.


For example, we hit a roadblock in my second paper where my findings changed the hypothesis to something we were less familiar with: microphase separation. At a national conference, I shared my progress and troubles, networked, and recruited collaborators with relevant expertise, such that, by combining my expertise in non-equilibrium systems and our collaborator's expertises in equilibrium mixtures, we were able to explain the emergent behavior and publish our work in ______.


Successes include enabling the design of experimental systems by deriving a theory that accurately predicts the final state of a system solely from our simulation inputs (experimentally measurable properties), mathematically proving how self-propulsion modifies the steady-state force balance that enables the cluster to form and be maintained, and mentoring new graduate and undergraduate students with multiple research projects. A grant proposal I wrote for this project was awarded the prestigious Department of Defense (DoD) National Defense Science and Engineering Graduate (NDSEG) Fellowship to fully fund three years of my graduate career.

Graduate Teaching Assistant: Data Science and Analytics

UNC Makerspaces: Be A Maker

Through front-end and back-end development, I designed various computational tools to increase makerspace users and facilitate a better user experience.


One challenge was leading teams of student employees with typically high turn-over on long-term projects. Most students fell in love with the makerspaces because of making something cool with their hands but that love can quickly fade when stocking hundreds of boxes of 3D printer resin or waiting in boredom for a customer to enter an empty makerspace. It was common for student employees to simply do menial tasks as they were told, neither realizing their importance nor feeling pride for their contributions. I made it a priority to continuously remind my team about the project's goals, their role in its success, and its importance and impact on others. I was proud that not only did all my team members continue working with me until our project's completion, but also many continued working with me on future projects.


Successes include designing an intuitive Tableau workbook that displays real-time user demographics analytics that enabled significantly more efficient staff scheduling and new marketing materials for advertising and funding purposes, creating databases and data collection routines for 3D printer and laser cutter user demographics, working with Salesforce to create an extensive inventory database and QR system accounting for product movement between four makerspaces, and developing and facilitating trainings for makerspace staff on inventory system usage.

Graduate Researcher: Experimental Physics (Perovskite Photodetectors)

UNC-Chapel Hill: Huang Group

I was responsible for performing self-directed research to experimentally design solution-processed, perovskite photodetectors.


Being the first student to research photodetectors in my lab, I built-up the lab’s capabilities to perform this research. I designed, built, and verified novel measurement systems to enable this research, such as a tool that measures the minimum intensity of detectible light. This was particularly challenging due to the measurement's incredible sensitivity to noise, so I had to be creative and precise with extreme noise mitigation techniques to make an accurate, cost-efficient, and user-friendly tool.


Successes included developing the first narrow-band perovskite photodetector for photoluminescence measurements, forming and managing a collaboration network between our lab and Brookhaven, Lawrence Livermore, and Savannah River National Laboratories, and writing a textbook chapter that summarizes past research and future directions to make perovskite photodetectors more accessible to scientists and students.

Research Intern: Computational Physics (Fusion Plasmas)

Wisconsin Plasma Physics Laboratory (WiPPL)

I developed a computational model in Python that accurately predicts the properties of fusion plasmas and the resulting energy emissions based on Bayesian statistics. By applying my computational model to determine the theoretically optimal design, I fabricated an x-ray detector that removes undesirable noise from other measurements, effectively increasing the measurement capabilities of scientists.


Before this position, I had never seen a line of computer code. Within this short period of time, I taught myself how to read and write complex code in both Python and IDL and applied this knowledge to design a physics-based model, whose accuracy was successfully verified experimentally.


Successes included increasing the Python model’s efficiency by 60% through replacing 1D lists and iterations over a loop with up to 6-dimensional tensor mathematics via NumPy, adding the capability to calculate the elemental impurity concentration profiles and measured signal of a simulated x-ray detector, and developing multi-variable Bayesian optimization routines to maximize the measurement capabilities of simulated x-ray detectors.

Teaching/Communication

STEM Mentor for High School Students

Junior Science and Humanities Symposium

I mentored highschool students across the world (based in the continental U.S., Puerto Rico, and Japan) on computational physics research projects for the annual Junior Science and Humanities Symposium and continue to provide long-term career mentorship for these STEM students.


Having students from diverse cultures and academic backgrounds, one particularly challenging yet incredibly rewarding aspect of this role was finding the best mentoring style to connect with each student and meet their individual needs and goals.


Successes include completing research projects that 1) apply machine learning to predict the elastic properties of materials, 2) use computer vision to track the motion of flies in petri dishes, and 3) developing an analytical program for determining optimal exoplanets for future habitation. One of my students placed third in the regional JSHS competition and first at the Puerto Rico State Science Fair, going on to compete at a national level at JSHS and the International Science and Engineering Fair (ISEF), which is the world's largest pre-college STEM competition.

Graduate Teaching Assistant

National Defense Science and Engineering Graduate (NDSEG) Research Fellowship Recipient

UNC-Chapel Hill: Klotsa Group

I was one of the 2% of 2021 applicants awarded the DoD NDSEG Fellowship, which provides $165,000 over 3 years to cover my tuition, stipend, healthcare, and travel expenses. The DoD NDSEG Fellowship Program is a competitive fellowship that is awarded to U.S. citizens, U.S. nationals, and U.S. dual citizens who intend to pursue a Doctoral degree aligned to the DoD services Broad Agency Announcements (BAAs) in research and development at a U.S. institution of their choice. My grant proposal on programmable multi-component active matter for crowd control applications was chosen to be supported by the Army Research Office (ARO).
