Active Matter

Active matter consists of systems of self-propelled individuals, such as humans walking, fish swimming, or colloids propelling from chemical reactions on their surfaces. Though these things look and move differently, they still exhibit similar, collective behavior: humans form crowds, fish form schools, and active colloids phase undergo a non-equilibrium phase separation into a dense, ordered cluster (bulk and interface) and a dilute, disorded gas. Self-propulsion enables these individuals to come together, interact, and act collectively, however, it also continuously drives the system out of equilibrium. Therefore, much of traditional physics (based on equilibrium systems) do not apply to these non-equilibrium systems, making it a challenge to understand why a system behaves as it does. My goal is to develop our understanding of active matter through computation to better enable the design of experimental systems and applications.

Phase behavior and surface tension of soft active Brownian particles

N. J. Lauersdorf, T. Kolb, M. Moradi, E. Nazockdast, and D. Klotsa. 2021 Soft Matter Emerging Investigators themed collection (invited) Soft Matter 17, 6337-6351 (2021)

I develop an analytical theory predicated on equilibrium statistical mechanics to predict the steady state of non-equilibrium, active Brownian (self-propelled) systems. Being able to predict the final, steady-state behavior of systems is typically reserved for traditional, equilibrium systems; therefore, knowing how our active systems, which appear much more chaotic, should collectively behave is incredibly unique, allowing for designing systems with a desired steady-state.

Perovskite Photodetectors

Recently, perovskites have gained much attention due to the unprecedented improvement of their ability to convert solar energy into electrical power. Many of the optoelectronic properties that make perovskite an ideal solar cell translate well to many other device applications, namely, photodetectors. A photodetector is a device that senses the presence of light of a specific wavelength. Next-generation photodetectors have gained increasing interest due to their widespread applications in image sensing, radiation detection, and fiber-optic communication. Perovskite, unlike most commercial detectors, is a solution-processed semiconductor, therefore, this detector can be mass-produced quickly and cheaply in almost the exact same method as newspapers: roll-to-roll printing, which makes it easily scalable and transportable.

Chapter 13: Perovskites enabled highly sensitive and fast photodetectors

N. J. Lauersdorf and J. Huang. Book chapter published in “Perovskite Photovoltaics and Optoelectronics: From Fundamentals to Advanced Applications” 383-409, edited by Tsutomu Miyasaka, Wiley (2021).

This textbook chapter distills perovskites and photodetectors to an easily-digestible manner for students and scientists new to or seeking to get into the field. I summarized the notable developments in the field of perovskite light detectors and provide my insight on fruitful research directions.

Tunable perovskite-based photodetectors in optical sensing

J. Wolanyk, X. Xiao, M. Fralaide, N. J. Lauersdorf, R. Kaudal, E. Dykstra, J. Huang, J. Shinar, and R. Shinar. Book chapter published in Sensors and Actuators. B 321, 1-7 (2020).

I developed the first narrow-band perovskite photodetector that can accurately differentiate photo-luminescent dyes, which is important for detecting harmful chemical or biological materials, such as COVID-19, in laboratory samples. Creating affordable detectors for this diagnostic tool, such as with perovskite instead of silicon, is essential for providing greater access to lower-income patients, especially in developing nations.

Fusion Plasmas

A fusion plasma is a concentrated soup of fast-moving ions and free electrons. As lighter ions interact, they combine to form heavier nucleii, releasing significant amounts of energy in the process. On Earth, researchers are trying to build fusion reactors to harness this virtually inexhaustible supply of energy from readily available elements. To do that, fusion reactors, such as the Madison Symmetric Torus (MST), heat plasmas to incredibly high temperatures and strongly confine the plasma via magnetic fields. However, current fusion generators are non-ideal for sustained fusion, i.e. MST plasmas have a signifiant number of aluminum impurities due to its aluminum vacuum vessel. Diagnostics allow us to understand the plasma properties, infer why the plasma possesses them, and design improvements for future iterations of fusion plasma experiments.

Development of a Ross Filter Based Aluminum Line Radiation (NickAl2) Detector in Madison Symmetric Torus

N. J. Lauersdorf, L. M. Reusch, and D. Den Hartog Senior thesis internally published by UW-Madison, 1-75 (2018).

When impurity ions interact with free electrons in the plasma, they produce x-ray radiation that complicated current diagnostics on MST. Applying a Python model that I helped develop, I simulated the energy emissions and measured signal from the Madison Symmetric Torus fusion plasma experiment to design and optimize a narrow-band (ROSS filter) x-ray detector that measures Aluminum line-radiation. Previously, diagnostics would use incredibly thick filters to block the Aluminum line radiation, losing significant information in the process. Now, measuring Aluminum line radiation directly enables all other measurements to use thinner filters and, therefore, retain significantly more information.
